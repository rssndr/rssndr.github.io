# The Biggest Enemy of Europe Isn't America or Russia

## Why performative anti-Trumpism and anti-Putinism won’t save our continent

Author: Andrea Rossetti <br>
Date: 30/04/2026 <br>
Published on: [Ave Atrium]()

---

For months now, a strange consensus has taken hold among many Europeans, including plenty of self-described "federalists", who should know better: every time Brussels, Paris, Berlin or Rome issues a statement criticising Donald Trump or Vladimir Putin, large parts of the commentariat fall over themselves to praise it. "Finally, Europe is standing up for itself!"

This is nonsense. And not only that: it's dangerous.

Yes, Trump’s threats and attacks and Putin’s aggression are real factors in the international environment, but treating opposition to them as a badge of European virtue is a catastrophic distraction. The single greatest threat to Europe’s security, prosperity and cohesion is not in Washington or Moscow: it is in our own capitals and institutions. Our ruling class is the primary reason Europe finds itself weak, divided and strategically irrelevant: politicians, commissioners, party machines and the ecosystem of NGOs and think-tanks that orbit them.

Look at the evidence. Putin has invaded Georgia, annexed Crimea, and launched a full-scale war against Ukraine; each time, the European response has been too little, too late, and too uncoordinated. The same pattern repeats with every external pressure. Our leaders prefer grand moral declarations over serious investment in defence, energy independence, or a credible common foreign policy; meanwhile, they largely ignore or downplay Europe’s own mounting crises.

The uncomfortable truth is that Europe’s ruling class is completely ineffective at solving (or even honestly addressing) our most pressing problems: demographic decline, uncontrolled mass migration, deindustrialisation, chronic energy vulnerabilities, housing shortages, and a defence industry still dangerously dependent on others. Instead of focusing on these internal failures, they often pretend Europe is a global power capable of intervening to solve all of the world’s problems. We are not. This performative activism on the global stage serves as a convenient distraction from our inability to secure even our own continent.

Seeking external enemies has become psychologically comfortable. Condemning Trump or Putin allows leaders and commentators to feel righteous and united without ever confronting the hard, unglamorous work of reform at home. It’s easier to rail against outsiders than to admit that the democratic deficit in EU decision-making, the capture of institutions by centrist cartels, the refusal to allow real competition of ideas, and the systematic sidelining of voters demanding real change are what have left us vulnerable in the first place. But this comfort is illusory. Every day spent chasing foreign villains instead of fixing ourselves makes our situation worse: more dependent, more divided, and less capable of shaping our own future.

This is not leadership, but delusion dressed up as virtue.

We cannot keep pretending that “more Europe” automatically means better Europe when the Europe we have is run by people who have repeatedly proven themselves incapable of delivering results. Praising the ruling class simply because it mouths the right slogans against the current American president or the Russian dictator is not patriotism, but complicity.

The way forward is painfully obvious, even if it is politically uncomfortable: Europe needs massive, structural reform. That means overhauling the EU’s governance to make it more accountable and more capable of acting decisively in a dangerous world. It means replacing the current political class with leaders who are willing to make hard choices on defence, energy, borders, innovation and fiscal responsibility instead of chasing applause in international forums and virtue-signalling on the global stage.

The current elites will not reform themselves. They have to be voted out systematically, relentlessly, and very soon. Only then can we begin the serious work of reclaiming and strengthening the European project.

Because the alternative is not a stronger Europe standing tall against Trump and Putin: it's a declining, fragmented continent carefully managing its own slow death — exactly what our ruling class has spent the last two decades delivering.

We still have time to change this. But first, we need to accept the uncomfortable truths we want so badly to avoid. The clock is ticking.
